## History of Derivatives

### Fermat (1629): Extrema Problems

Fermat studied function extrema by setting f(x) and f(x+E) equal, then letting E approach 0. He was essentially finding where the derivative is zero, though the concept didn't yet exist.

### Newton (1665): Fluxions

Newton called variables "fluents" and their rates of change "fluxions." He used dotted notation: $$\dot{x}$$ for the fluxion of x (dx/dt). With fluxions, he computed planetary orbits and tidal patterns.

### Leibniz (1675): A Symbol Revolution

Leibniz independently invented calculus and designed the dy/dx and ∫ notation we still use. His symbols were more intuitive: dy/dx explicitly shows the rate of change of y with respect to x.

> The Newton-Leibniz priority dispute over who invented calculus first persisted for decades. Today both are recognized as independent discoverers.

### Lagrange (1797): Purely Algebraic

Lagrange tried to define derivatives entirely through Taylor series: f'(x) is the coefficient of h in f(x+h) = f(x) + f'(x)h + ... He sidestepped infinitesimals entirely.

### Cauchy (1823): The Modern Definition

Cauchy defined the derivative on the foundation of limits: $$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$$. Derivatives no longer relied on "infinitesimal" intuition but on rigorous limit theory.

### Late 19th Century: Differential Geometry

Gauss and Riemann extended derivatives from one dimension to higher dimensions, using partial derivatives and gradients to describe curved surfaces. Einstein's 1915 general relativity is, at its core, a description of spacetime geometry using these tools.
