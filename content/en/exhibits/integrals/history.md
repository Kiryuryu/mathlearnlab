## The Story of Integration

### 250 BCE: Archimedes Exhausts a Parabola

Archimedes filled a parabolic segment with triangles. He cleverly chose triangles so that each step left 1/4 of the remaining area, then summed the series for an exact result. Integration's first great triumph.

### 200 BCE: Liu Hui's Circle Method

Chinese mathematician Liu Hui approximated circles with regular polygons — starting with a hexagon and doubling sides each step. He reached 3072 sides, obtaining π ≈ 3.1416. This was essentially approximating the integral $\int \sqrt{1-x^2}dx$.

### 1615: Kepler's Wine Barrels

Calculating wine cask volumes, Kepler pioneered the "summation of infinitesimal slices" method. He cut the barrel into infinitely many thin disks, approximated each as a cylinder, and summed them. His work inspired the integral concept.

### 1666: Newton's Reverse Fluxion

Newton noticed that the inverse of differentiation (finding the original quantity from its rate of change) exactly computes the area under a curve. This was the germ of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

### 1675: Leibniz's ∫ Symbol

Leibniz introduced ∫ — a elongated S for "Summa" — representing infinite summation. He emphasized dx not as a meaningless symbol but as each rectangle's width.

### 1854: Riemann's Rigorous Definition

Riemann first rigorously defined the definite integral: partition the interval into n pieces, pick a sample point in each, sum, and let n → ∞. When the limit exists regardless of sample point choice, it's the Riemann integral. This is what you see when you drag the n slider.

### 1902: Lebesgue's Measure

Lebesgue sliced the area by function values rather than x-axis positions. The Lebesgue integral handles more general functions and forms the mathematical foundation of probability theory and functional analysis.
