## The Story of Limits

### 5th Century BCE: Zeno's Paradoxes

Zeno's "flying arrow" paradox: at every instant, an arrow occupies a fixed position. If it's motionless at each instant, how can it move?

This forced the first seeds of limit thinking — motion and change required a new mathematical language.

### 3rd Century BCE: Archimedes' Method of Exhaustion

Archimedes filled a parabolic region with infinitely many triangles, proving its area equals 4/3 times the inscribed triangle. This was the first clear application of limit reasoning.

### 17th Century: Intuition Triumphs

Newton and Leibniz built calculus using "infinitesimals" — quantities smaller than any positive number but not zero. Despite logical gaps, this intuitive system launched the physics revolution.

### 18th Century: Berkeley's Critique

Bishop Berkeley attacked: "Infinitesimals are the ghosts of departed quantities." If they are zero, everything is zero; if non-zero, the calculations are inexact.

### 19th Century: Cauchy-Weierstrass Rigor

Cauchy, in his 1821 *Cours d'Analyse*, first defined limits using "values that approach a fixed value." Weierstrass gave the final ε-δ definition — limits no longer relied on intuition but on pure logic.

> A two-thousand-year struggle to precisely describe "arbitrarily close" was finally resolved.
