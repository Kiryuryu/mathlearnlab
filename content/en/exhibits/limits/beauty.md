## Beauty in Limits

### The Most Beautiful Limit

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n = e$$

This defines e (≈ 2.71828) as the "limit of instant-by-instant growth." If you have $1 at 100% annual interest compounded continuously, you'll have $e after one year.

### sin(x)/x → 1

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1$$

When the angle is tiny, arc length and chord length are nearly equal. This simple fact is the foundation of all trigonometric calculus. Without it, there would be no derivative of sin(x), and no mathematical description of vibrations, waves, or AC electricity.

### The Basel Problem

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = 1 + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{9} + \frac{1}{16} + \cdots = \frac{\pi^2}{6}$$

The sum of reciprocal squares of natural numbers equals π²/6 — two seemingly unrelated constants meeting here. Euler spent 7 years on this.

### The Golden Ratio Limit

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{F_{n+1}}{F_n} = \varphi = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 1.618$$

The ratio of consecutive Fibonacci numbers (1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, ...) approaches the golden ratio. This proportion appears in sunflower seed patterns, nautilus shells, and even galactic arms.

### Stolz–Cesàro Theorem

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_{n+1}-a_n}{b_{n+1}-b_n}$$

A discrete version of L'Hôpital's rule — replacing derivatives with differences, building a bridge between discrete and continuous limits.
