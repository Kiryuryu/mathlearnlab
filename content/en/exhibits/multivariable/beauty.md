## Beauty in Multivariable Calculus

### Green's Theorem

$$\oint_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right) dx\,dy$$

The line integral around a closed curve equals the double integral over the interior region. A duality between boundary and interior — "look at the edge, know the inside."

### Stokes' Theorem

$$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S}$$

The circulation along a closed curve bounding a surface equals the flux of the curl through that surface. Green's theorem and Gauss's theorem are both special cases — three theorems are really one theorem expressed in different dimensions.

### The Divergence Theorem (Gauss's Theorem)

$$\iiint_V \nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}\,dV = \oiint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}$$

The total "source strength" inside a volume equals the flux through its surface. This explains why heat radiates from a source and why current flows through a conductor.

### Saddle Points

z = x² - y² forms a saddle surface. At the origin, it's convex in the x-direction and concave in the y-direction. The discriminant fxx·fyy - fxy² < 0 identifies a saddle point — neither maximum nor minimum. Like a mountain pass: uphill in one direction, downhill in another.

### The Hairy Ball Theorem

Multivariable calculus proves you cannot define a continuous non-vanishing tangent vector field on a sphere. In plain terms: you can't perfectly comb a hairy ball flat — there will always be two "cowlicks" (at the poles). This relates to topology's Euler characteristic.
