## The Story of Multivariable Calculus

### 1660s: The Germ of Partial Derivatives

Newton, deriving universal gravitation, needed to consider how forces vary in three spatial directions. Though he never explicitly named "partial derivatives," his calculations contained the seeds of multivariable thinking.

### 1736: Euler's Calculus of Variations

Euler, studying the brachistochrone problem (what curve gives the fastest descent?), pioneered the calculus of variations — finding extrema of functionals. This is essentially calculus in infinite-dimensional space.

### 1760: Lagrange's Multipliers

Lagrange invented a method for constrained optimization: to find an extremum of f(x,y) under g(x,y) = 0, construct L(x,y,λ) = f - λg and set ∇L = 0. This elegantly turns a constrained problem into an unconstrained one.

### 1788: Laplace's Equation

∇²φ = 0 — this terse PDE describes steady-state temperature, electric potential, and fluid potential. Laplace proposed it while studying planetary gravitation, and it later appeared in nearly every branch of physics.

### 1828: Green's Theorem

A self-taught English miller, George Green, published his only mathematical paper, introducing Green's theorem and Green's functions. The paper went largely unnoticed at the time, later becoming foundational for electromagnetism and quantum mechanics.

### 1854: Riemann's Curved Spaces

In his inaugural lecture "On the Hypotheses Which Lie at the Bases of Geometry," Riemann extended Gauss's surface theory to n-dimensional curved spaces. He defined the curvature tensor describing how space bends. Half a century later, Einstein used this framework for general relativity.

### 1915: Einstein's Field Equations

The Einstein field equations: Rμν - (1/2)Rgμν = 8πTμν. Left side: spacetime curvature (pure geometry). Right side: matter-energy distribution (physics). Every term contains partial derivatives — spacetime curvature is described by multivariable calculus.
