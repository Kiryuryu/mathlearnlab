## Beauty in Series

### The Basel Problem

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = 1 + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{9} + \cdots = \frac{\pi^2}{6}$$

Euler's 1735 proof is among the most ingenious in mathematics: he wrote sin(x) as an infinite product (like factoring a polynomial), then compared coefficients to obtain π²/6.

### The Harmonic Series Surprise

The harmonic series $$\sum \frac{1}{n}$$ diverges (slowly climbing to infinity), but remove all terms containing the digit 9, and it converges! Because as n grows, the proportion of numbers containing 9 approaches 100%.

### Leibniz Series

$$\frac{\pi}{4} = 1 - \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{5} - \frac{1}{7} + \frac{1}{9} - \cdots$$

An alternating series equals π/4. It converges excruciatingly slowly — hundreds of thousands of terms for 5-digit accuracy. But its beauty is that π hides in a simple alternating sum.

### The Zeta Function

ζ(s) = 1 + 2^{-s} + 3^{-s} + 4^{-s} + ... For s = 2, this is the Basel problem. Riemann extended it to complex numbers, discovering the still-unproven Riemann Hypothesis — the secret of prime distribution lies in the zeros of ζ(s).

### Fourier's Philosophy

Any periodic function can be written as an infinite sum of sine waves. This claim was fiercely opposed by Lagrange and others when Fourier first proposed it in 1807. Fourier persisted, and two centuries later, his method became the mathematical engine of the information age.
