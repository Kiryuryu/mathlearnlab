## The Story of Series

### 5th Century BCE: Zeno's Dichotomy

"To reach the finish, you must first get halfway; to get halfway, you must first get a quarter..." Zeno's dichotomy paradox is essentially the infinite series 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + ... = 1.

### 14th Century: Oresme's Harmonic Series

French scholar Nicole Oresme first proved the harmonic series 1 + 1/2 + 1/3 + ... diverges. He cleverly grouped terms so each group sums to more than 1/2, yielding infinitely many halves — this proof remains a textbook classic.

### 1665: Newton's Binomial Series

Newton extended the binomial theorem (1+x)ⁿ to fractional exponents. He discovered that (1+x)^{1/2} can be expressed as an infinite series — a precursor to Taylor series.

### 1735: Euler Solves the Basel Problem

Euler, with stunning intuition, treated sin(x) as an infinite polynomial and derived the exact sum of the Basel problem: π²/6. Though his proof lacked rigor (he later filled the gaps), the result stunned all of Europe.

### 1807: Fourier's Rebellion

Fourier submitted a paper to the Paris Academy claiming "any function can be represented by trigonometric series." Lagrange objected on the spot — it wasn't rigorous. Fourier persisted, publishing *The Analytical Theory of Heat* 15 years later, and was ultimately proven right.

### 1830: Abel's Convergence Theory

At age 22, Abel became the first to rigorously study power series convergence. The concept of the radius of convergence was born — series don't just "work" at certain points; they have a well-defined "range of validity."

### 1854: The Cauchy-Hadamard Theorem

Cauchy and Hadamard gave the precise formula for a power series's radius of convergence, revealing that every power series has a "disk" (in the complex plane) — converging inside, diverging outside.

### The Riemann Hypothesis (1859–present)

Riemann extended the series ζ(s) to complex numbers. He found that the non-trivial zeros of ζ might all lie on a single line — if proven, our understanding of primes would be revolutionized. This problem carries a $1M prize and remains unsolved.
